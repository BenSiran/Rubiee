IELTS Speaking Test - Complete Guide

Master all three parts with expert strategies and practice

Welcome to Your IELTS Speaking Journey

This comprehensive guide covers everything you need to excel in the IELTS Speaking test. The test lasts 11-14 minutes and consists of three distinct parts.

You'll engage in a face-to-face interview with an examiner who will assess your ability to communicate effectively in English across various contexts.

Navigate through each section using the menu above to explore sample questions, model answers, and practical exercises designed to help you achieve your target band score.

Test Structure Overview

  • Part 1 (4-5 minutes): Introduction and general questions about yourself and familiar topics
  • Part 2 (3-4 minutes): Long turn where you speak for 1-2 minutes on a given topic
  • Part 3 (4-5 minutes): Two-way discussion with more abstract questions related to Part 2

PART 1: Introduction and Interview

Duration: 4-5 minutes | Focus: General questions about yourself and familiar topics

SET 1: Home and Accommodation

1. Do you live in a house or an apartment?
"I currently live in an apartment in the city center. It's a two-bedroom flat on the fifth floor of a modern building, which I share with a roommate."
2. What do you like most about where you live?
"What I appreciate most is the location. It's extremely convenient because I'm within walking distance of shops, restaurants, and public transportation. The neighborhood is also quite safe and has a friendly atmosphere."
3. How long have you lived there?
"I've been living there for about two years now, ever since I started my university studies. Time has really flown by, and I've grown quite comfortable with the area."
4. Would you like to move to a different place in the future?
"Yes, eventually I would. While I enjoy the convenience of city living, I'd prefer to move to a quieter suburban area in the future, perhaps somewhere with a small garden. I think it would be better for raising a family."

SET 2: Work or Studies

1. What do you do? Do you work or are you a student?
"I'm currently a student at the local university, studying Business Administration. I'm in my third year, so I'll be graduating next summer."
2. Why did you choose this job/course?
"I chose this course because I've always been interested in how companies operate and make strategic decisions. My parents also run a small business, so I grew up watching them manage various aspects of their company, which sparked my interest in this field."

SET 3: Hobbies and Interests

1. What do you like to do in your free time?
"In my leisure time, I really enjoy reading, particularly historical fiction and biographies. I also like to play tennis on weekends with friends, which helps me stay active and socialize."
2. How often do you do this activity?
"I try to read for at least 30 minutes every evening before bed. It's become a relaxing ritual that helps me unwind. As for tennis, I play once or twice a week, depending on my schedule and the weather."

Be natural: Speak as you would in a normal conversation, but maintain good grammar.

Stay relevant: Keep your answers focused on the question asked.

PART 3: Two-Way Discussion

Duration: 4-5 minutes | Focus: Abstract questions related to Part 2 topic

Discussion 1: Travel and Tourism

How has tourism changed over the past few decades?
"Tourism has undergone dramatic transformations in recent decades, primarily due to technological advances and globalization. Perhaps the most significant change is accessibility‚Äîbudget airlines and online booking platforms have made international travel affordable for middle-class families, not just the wealthy.

Technology has also revolutionized how people plan and experience travel. Travelers now research destinations thoroughly online, read reviews, and create detailed itineraries before departing. However, this increase in tourism has created challenges. Many popular destinations suffer from over-tourism, which damages local infrastructure and environments."
What are the advantages and disadvantages of international travel?
"International travel offers numerous advantages. Culturally, it broadens perspectives by exposing people to different ways of life, challenging their assumptions and reducing prejudices. Economically, tourism creates employment opportunities in developing nations and facilitates cultural exchange.

However, there are notable disadvantages. Environmentally, the carbon footprint of air travel contributes significantly to climate change. Culturally, mass tourism can lead to the commodification of local traditions. The key is finding balance‚Äîtraveling responsibly, supporting local economies, and being mindful of our environmental and cultural impact."

Discussion 2: Technology and Society

How has technology changed the way people communicate?
"Technology has fundamentally revolutionized human communication in both positive and transformative ways. The most obvious change is the speed and reach of communication. We can now instantaneously connect with people anywhere in the world through text, voice, or video.

However, these technological advances have also altered the quality of communication. Digital communication often lacks the non-verbal cues‚Äîfacial expressions, body language, tone‚Äîthat provide context and emotional nuance to interactions. The challenge moving forward is leveraging technology's benefits while preserving the depth and authenticity of human connection."

üí° Part 3 Tips

Think critically: Present multiple perspectives and analyze issues in depth.

Use discourse markers: However, moreover, on the other hand, consequently...

Provide examples: Support your points with real-world examples.

Don't rush: Take a moment to think before answering complex questions.

FINAL TIPS FROM INSTRUCTOR BEN SIRAN

Essential strategies for success in your IELTS Speaking test

üéì Top 10 Success Strategies

  • Practice Regularly: Consistency is more important than marathon study sessions. Practice speaking English daily, even if just for 15 minutes.
  • Record Yourself: This helps you identify areas for improvement that you might not notice while speaking.
  • Expand Your Answers: Never give one-word or single-sentence answers. Use the "PREP" method: Point, Reason, Example, Personal experience.
  • Stay Calm: The examiner wants you to succeed. If you don't understand a question, politely ask for clarification.
  • Be Yourself: The test assesses your English ability, not whether you have impressive experiences or strong opinions.
  • Think in English: Try to avoid translating from your native language, as this slows you down and can lead to errors.
  • Use Signposting Language: Help the examiner follow your thoughts with phrases like "First of all," "Another point is," and "To sum up."
  • Manage Your Time: In Part 2, if you finish early, use remaining time to elaborate rather than sitting in silence.
  • Show Range: Demonstrate your English abilities by using varied vocabulary, different tenses, and complex structures when appropriate.
  • Stay Positive: Your attitude matters. Approach the test as an opportunity to showcase your English skills.
